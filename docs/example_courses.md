# Example Courses

A reference list of 30 courses suitable for the platform, spanning programming, creative skills, science, history, practical life skills, and more.

---

## Programming & Technology

### 1. Programming in Elixir
An introduction to functional programming using Elixir. Covers the basics of the language, pattern matching, processes, OTP, and building a simple concurrent application.

**Topics:** What is Elixir and why use it, installing and using IEx, basic types and operators, pattern matching, functions and modules, recursion, lists and the pipe operator, processes and message passing, intro to OTP and GenServer, building a small project

---

### 2. Python for Beginners
A from-scratch introduction to Python. No prior programming experience required. Covers core language concepts through practical examples and small projects.

**Topics:** What is programming, setting up Python, variables and data types, strings and string methods, lists and loops, conditionals, functions, dictionaries, reading and writing files, a small final project

---

### 3. How the Web Works
A conceptual course explaining what happens when you type a URL into a browser. Covers DNS, HTTP, servers, clients, and the basics of HTML/CSS/JavaScript without requiring any coding.

**Topics:** Clients and servers, what is a URL, DNS — the internet's phone book, HTTP requests and responses, what browsers actually do, HTML in plain English, CSS in plain English, JavaScript in plain English, cookies and sessions, HTTPS and why it matters

---

### 4. Introduction to SQL
Learn to query databases using SQL. Covers selecting, filtering, joining, and aggregating data using real-world examples.

**Topics:** What is a database, tables, rows and columns, your first SELECT query, filtering with WHERE, sorting with ORDER BY, aggregate functions, GROUP BY, joining tables, subqueries and common patterns

---

### 5. Git and Version Control
Understand how Git works and why version control matters. Covers the core Git workflow, branching, merging, and collaborating on GitHub.

**Topics:** Why version control exists, installing Git and first config, init, add, and commit, reading the log, branching, merging and resolving conflicts, remote repositories, push and pull, pull requests on GitHub, a practical team workflow

---

## Science & Nature

### 6. How Volcanoes Work
An earth science course covering the geology behind volcanoes, how they form, how they erupt, and their role in shaping the planet.

**Topics:** What is the Earth made of, tectonic plates, hotspots and subduction zones, types of volcanoes, types of eruptions, what comes out of a volcano, famous eruptions in history, volcanic islands, predicting eruptions, volcanoes and climate

---

### 7. Introduction to Astronomy
A beginner's guide to the night sky and the science behind it. Covers stars, planets, galaxies, and the tools astronomers use.

**Topics:** The scale of the universe, stars and their life cycles, our solar system, planets and moons, the Moon, telescopes and how they work, reading a star chart, constellations, black holes, galaxies and the expanding universe

---

### 8. How Evolution Works
A clear explanation of Darwin's theory of evolution by natural selection, the evidence behind it, and common misconceptions.

**Topics:** What evolution is (and isn't), natural selection explained, variation and inheritance, genetics and DNA basics, the fossil record, speciation, common descent, human evolution, evolutionary misconceptions, evolution in everyday life

---

### 9. Introduction to Marine Biology
Explore the ocean's ecosystems, the organisms that live in them, and the science used to study them.

**Topics:** Ocean zones and environments, photosynthesis and the food web, plankton — the base of everything, coral reefs, deep sea ecosystems, fish anatomy and behaviour, marine mammals, ocean threats and conservation, how marine biologists work, careers in marine science

---

### 10. The Human Body
A non-technical overview of the major systems of the human body — how they work individually and how they interact.

**Topics:** Cells and tissues, the skeletal system, muscles and movement, the cardiovascular system, the respiratory system, the digestive system, the nervous system, the immune system, hormones and the endocrine system, how the body repairs itself

---

## History & Society

### 11. The Cold War
A narrative course covering the origins, key events, and eventual end of the Cold War between the US and Soviet Union.

**Topics:** Origins after World War II, the Iron Curtain, the arms race, Korea and the first proxy war, McCarthyism, the space race, the Cuban Missile Crisis, Vietnam, détente and Nixon, the fall of the Berlin Wall

---

### 12. The History of Money
How money evolved from barter to coins to paper to digital, and what that tells us about society and power.

**Topics:** Barter and its limits, the first coins, paper money in China, the gold standard, central banks, the Bretton Woods system, fiat currency, the 2008 financial crisis, cryptocurrency, the future of money

---

### 13. World War I Explained
A clear account of how the First World War started, how it was fought, and what it changed about the world.

**Topics:** Europe before 1914, the assassination of Franz Ferdinand, why war spread so fast, trench warfare, the major fronts, technology that changed warfare, the home front, the US enters the war, armistice and the Treaty of Versailles, the war's long shadow

---

### 14. How Democracies Work
A civics course explaining the structures, principles, and tensions within democratic systems of government.

**Topics:** What democracy means, direct vs representative democracy, constitutions and the rule of law, separation of powers, elections and voting systems, political parties, the role of a free press, courts and judicial independence, how laws are made, threats to democracy

---

### 15. The History of the Internet
From ARPANET to the smartphone era — a social and technical history of how the internet was built and how it changed everything.

**Topics:** Cold War origins and ARPANET, early email and bulletin boards, Tim Berners-Lee and the World Wide Web, browsers and the dot-com boom, Google and the search era, social media, the smartphone revolution, the platform economy, surveillance capitalism, what comes next

---

## Creative Skills

### 16. Introduction to Music Theory
The fundamentals of how music works — notes, scales, chords, and rhythm — explained clearly without assuming any instrument knowledge.

**Topics:** What is sound, notes and the musical alphabet, the piano keyboard as a map, scales — major and minor, intervals, chords and how they're built, chord progressions, rhythm and time signatures, reading a lead sheet, putting it all together

---

### 17. Photography Fundamentals
Learn how cameras work and how to take better photographs. Covers the exposure triangle, composition, and light.

**Topics:** How a camera sees, the exposure triangle — aperture, shutter speed, ISO, depth of field, composition rules and when to break them, understanding light, shooting in different conditions, white balance and colour, editing basics, developing your eye

---

### 18. How to Draw in Perspective
A practical drawing course covering the core techniques of perspective — from one-point basics to complex interior and exterior scenes.

**Topics:** What perspective actually is, the horizon line and vanishing points, one-point perspective, two-point perspective, drawing boxes and buildings, interior spaces, three-point perspective, ellipses and cylinders, common mistakes, putting it into practice

---

### 19. Introduction to Graphic Design
The principles behind good visual design — layout, colour, typography, and hierarchy — for beginners with no prior design experience.

**Topics:** What graphic design is for, visual hierarchy, the grid system, colour theory basics, working with type, contrast and space, using imagery, logo design basics, designing for print vs screen, looking at design critically

---

### 20. Creative Writing Fundamentals
An introduction to the craft of fiction — character, voice, structure, dialogue, and the habits of a working writer.

**Topics:** Why people write and read fiction, finding your voice, character and motivation, point of view, showing vs telling, writing dialogue, scene structure, pacing, revision as a process, building a writing habit

---

## Practical & Life Skills

### 21. Personal Finance Fundamentals
A practical guide to managing money — budgeting, debt, saving, and investing — without jargon.

**Topics:** Why most people struggle with money, building a budget, emergency funds, good debt vs bad debt, understanding credit scores, compound interest and why time matters, intro to investing, index funds explained, pensions and retirement basics, financial habits that stick

---

### 22. How to Read a Scientific Paper
A meta-skills course for anyone who wants to engage with scientific research critically and confidently.

**Topics:** Why this skill matters, the anatomy of a paper, understanding abstracts, reading methods critically, what statistics actually tell you, peer review — what it means and doesn't mean, spotting red flags in research, preprints and the publication pipeline, science journalism vs science, building a reading practice

---

### 23. Cooking Fundamentals
Not recipes — the underlying techniques and knowledge that make you a better cook regardless of what you're making.

**Topics:** Heat and how it changes food, the five mother sauces, knife skills and safety, seasoning at every stage, the Maillard reaction, cooking proteins, emulsification, stocks and broths, balancing flavour, how to improvise from what you have

---

### 24. Introduction to Nutrition
The science of what food does in the body — macros, micros, metabolism, and how to think critically about nutrition claims.

**Topics:** What food is made of, carbohydrates and energy, protein and muscle, fats — the good and the complicated, vitamins and minerals, how digestion works, reading a nutrition label, metabolism and body weight, evaluating nutrition advice, eating patterns that have evidence behind them

---

### 25. How to Learn Anything
A course on learning itself — the cognitive science behind how memory and skill acquisition work, and how to apply it.

**Topics:** How memory works, the forgetting curve, spaced repetition, active recall vs passive review, interleaving, the role of sleep, deliberate practice, mental models, learning a new skill from scratch, building a personal learning system

---

## Philosophy & Critical Thinking

### 26. Introduction to Logic and Argumentation
Learn to construct and evaluate arguments clearly. Covers deductive and inductive reasoning, logical form, and common fallacies.

**Topics:** What an argument is, premises and conclusions, deductive validity, inductive strength, logical form, modus ponens and tollens, common formal fallacies, common informal fallacies, spotting bad arguments in the wild, building a sound argument

---

### 27. Introduction to Philosophy
A tour through the biggest questions in Western philosophy — knowledge, ethics, mind, and existence — using primary thinkers as guides.

**Topics:** What philosophy is for, Plato and the theory of Forms, Descartes and radical doubt, Hume and the problem of induction, Kant's Copernican revolution, utilitarianism — Bentham and Mill, Nietzsche and morality, existentialism, philosophy of mind, does ethics have answers

---

### 28. How to Think About Risk
The psychology and mathematics of risk — why humans are bad at probability and how to think more clearly about uncertainty.

**Topics:** What risk actually is, probability intuition and why it fails, base rate neglect, the availability heuristic, loss aversion, expected value, black swans and fat tails, risk in everyday decisions, communicating risk, calibrated uncertainty

---

## Economics

### 29. Economics for Everyone
The core ideas in economics explained plainly — supply and demand, markets, incentives, and how economies are managed.

**Topics:** What economics studies, scarcity and trade-offs, supply and demand, price signals, market failures, externalities, public goods, how firms make decisions, labour markets, GDP and how economies are measured, inflation and monetary policy

---

### 30. Behavioural Economics
How psychology and economics overlap — why people make irrational decisions and what that means for policy, business, and everyday life.

**Topics:** The limits of rational actor theory, heuristics and biases, prospect theory and loss aversion, anchoring and framing, the endowment effect, social norms and behaviour, nudge theory, choice architecture, behavioural economics in the real world, critiques and limits of the field

---

*Total: 30 courses across 7 domains — Programming & Technology (5), Science & Nature (5), History & Society (5), Creative Skills (5), Practical & Life Skills (5), Philosophy & Critical Thinking (3), Economics (2).*
