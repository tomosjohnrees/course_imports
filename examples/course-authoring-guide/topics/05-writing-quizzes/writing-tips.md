# Tips for Writing Good Quizzes

Well-crafted quizzes reinforce learning. Poorly written ones frustrate learners. Here are some guidelines.

- **Use plausible distractors.** Every wrong option should sound reasonable to someone who has not fully understood the material. Avoid joke answers or obviously wrong choices — they make the quiz too easy and teach nothing.
- **Keep options similar in length and style.** If the correct answer is a long, detailed sentence and every distractor is two words, the learner can guess without thinking.
- **Avoid "all of the above" or "none of the above."** These options are ambiguous and often lead to arguments rather than learning.
- **Test one concept per question.** Compound questions that mix two ideas make it hard for learners to identify what they got wrong.
- **Write clear, unambiguous questions.** Read the question as if you know nothing about the topic. Could it be misinterpreted?
- **Place quizzes after the relevant content.** A quiz should appear after the material it tests, not before.
- **Use explanations liberally.** Even when the learner answers correctly, a brief explanation reinforces why the answer is correct.
